Financial Instruments

Financial Market and Instruments.

The money market is the market for short-time, low-risk, highly liquid security. “Short-term” refers to money market securities having maturities of less then one year to as short as overnight.“Low risk” specifically means that the probability of default by the issuer(where issuer failed to fail meet the obligations) is very unlikely.

  • Money market securities are homogeneous.
  • High liquidity means money market securities sold in secondary market very quickly.
  • Transactions in money market are typically vary large in value,often involving institutional players like banks, financial institution, corporations, and governments
  • In India, the money market is primarily regulated by Reserve Bank of India(RBI), which uses it as a key tool for implementing monetary policy.

Money Market Instruments.

These are the specific financial tools or securities traded in the money market.They are debt instrument, means they represent a loan from the investor to the issuer.

Here are most common money market instruments:-

  1. Treasury Bills(T-bills)

    • Issued by the government of India
    • They are zero coupon instrument, meaning they don’t pay interest directly. Instead, they are sold at a discount to their face value and redeemed at par(face value) upon maturity.
    • Typically issued for 91 days, 182 days, 364 days
    • Basically it’s risk-free.

  2. Commercial Paper(CP)

    • Unsecured promissory notes issued by large, creditworthy corporations and financial institutions.
    • Used by companies to raise short-term funds for working capital needs, financing receivables. Also issued at a discount price.
    • Rangers from 7 to 1 year
    • Caries some risk because it’s unsecure and depends of creditworthiness of issuer.Only companies with strong credit worthiness can issue CP.

  3. Certificates of Deposit(CDs)

    • CDs offered by banks and financial institutions.
    • A CD is a receipt for funds deposited in a bank for a fixed period at a fixed interest rate. While retail CDs are common, large-denominations (e.g., ₹1 lakh and more) are actively traded in money market by institutional investors.
    • Ranges from 7 to 1 year(for banks), or up to 3 years for financial institutions.
    • Relatively low risk.

  4. Repurchase Agreements (Repos) \ Reverse Repo

    • A very short-term(often overnight) borrowings arrangements. One party sells a security(often government bonds) to another party with an agreement to repurchase it at slightly higher price on specified future date.
    • Borrower’s perspective(Repo): Sells securities to raise cash, agreeing to buy them later.
    • Lender’s perspective(Reverse Repo): Buys securities by lending cash, agreeing to sell them later.
    • Typically overnight, but can extend for few days or weeks.

  5. Call money / Term money / Notice money

    • These are inter banks loans (loans between banks). Banks with surplus cash lends to banks with temporary shortage of cash.
    • Call money Loans are for 1 days(repayable on demand)
    • Notice money Loans are for a period of 2 to 14 days.
    • Term money Loans are for period of 15 days up to 1 year.
    • Primarily credit risk for borrowing banks.

  6. Banker’s Acceptance(BAs)

    • A time draft (promise to pay a certain sum on future date) that is accepted by the bank.
    • Used to facilitate International Trade transactions (Imports and Exports) where there’s trust gap between buyer and seller.
    • Typically 30 to 180 days
    • Low risk due to banker’s grantee.

Capital Market and Instruments

The capital market is the market for long-term financial instruments.The capital market is similar to money market, however maturity time is longer, default risk varies from low to high, and liquidity is less certain, as in homogeneity of the financial instrument. Broadly, we separate capital market instrument into debt instrument traded in bond market and equity securities traded in stock market.

Capital market is divided into two main components:-

  1. Primary Market: Where new securities issue for the first time.
  2. Secondary Market: Where existing securities are traded among investors (e.g., stock exchange like BSE or NSE)
  • The primary purpose is capital formation.It allows companies to raise necessary funds for large-scale projects.
  • Capital market instrument generally have high risk-return profile than money market instrument.

Capital Market Instruments

A. Equity Instruments (Ownership)

  1. Equity Shares(common stock)

    • The most common type of equity. Each shares represent fractional ownership of company.
    • Shareholders have voting rights within the company( like electing board of members). They are entitled to of the company’s profits in the form of dividends , but these are not guaranteed shareholders only receive dividend after debt settlements.
    • Risk risk-return, as their value can fluctuate dramatically with company performance and market conditions.

  2. Preference Stock (Preferred stock)

    • A hybrid instrument with feature of both debt and equity.
    • No voting rights.They receive fixed dividend that must be paid before any dividend can be paid to equity shareholders.
    • Lower risk and return than equity share, but higher than bonds.

B. Debt Instruments (Loans)

  1. Bonds

    • A long-term debut instrument issued by government and corporations.
    • A bond is basically a promise by the issuer to pay the investor a fixed of variable interest rate (coupon) for a specified period and to replay the face value (principal) on a set date.
    • Lower risk than stocks but also lower potential return

  2. Debentures

    • Similar to corporate bonds, but they are typically unsecured in most jurisdictions.This means they are not backed by any specific asset or collateral of the company.
    • Higher risk then secured bonds ,as the debenture holder is general creditor in case of default.
    • Can be convertible (can be converted into equity share ) or non-convertible

  3. Warrants

    • A warrant is a derivative that gives the holder the rights, but not the obligations, to purchase a specific number of shares at a predetermined price for a specific period
    • Warrants are often issued alongside bonds or preferred shares to make them more attractive to investors.
    • They are speculative and have high-risk profile, smiler to options.

C. Other Capital Market Instruments.

  1. Mutual Funds

    • A mutual funds pools money from multiple investors to invest in diversified portfolio of securities.(stocks,bonds, money instrument, etc.)
    • Providers professional management, diversification, and accessibility for retail investors who may have not have the capital or expertise to invest directly.
    • Risk-return depends entirely on underlying assets of the fund(e.g.,equity mutual fund is high risk, debt fund is mid-low risk.

  2. Exchange Traded Funds(ETFs)

    • Similar to mutual funds, but they are traded on a stock exchange throughout the day, just like individual stock.
    • Benefit of ETFs is they track a specific index (e.g., Nifty 50 ETF), providing instant diversification at very low cost.

  3. Derivatives

    • Derivatives are financial contract (like future and options) who value is derived from an underlying asset (like a stock or index)
    • Used for hedging risk, speculating on price movement, and arbitrage
    • Extremely high risk and potential return. Not for beginners.
