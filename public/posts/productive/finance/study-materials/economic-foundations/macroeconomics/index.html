Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics looks at economy as a whole. It focuses on broad issues such as inflation, unemployment, and growth of production. When the mangers of an automotive company look at the prices of steel and how they impact their production’s cost, they are looking at macroeconomic issue. Rather then being concerned about a single industry or company, microeconomics is a branch of economic theory that concerns with overall environments where businesses operates.

Inflation

When you hear your parents talk about how much they paid for their first automobile. Or maybe your grandparents reminisce about spending a quarter to purchase Coke. These conversations often turns into discussions about how dollar doesn’t go far as used to go. The reason for this is inflation or, a general increase in price level.

How is Inflation measured?

The measurement of inflation is conducted through two primary indices, each serving different purpose within the economic framework. These indices are Consumer Price Index(CPI) and the Wholesale Price Index(WPI)

The Consumer Price Index(CPI) is a measure of average change over time in the prices paid by urban and rural consumers for a basket of consumer goods & services. The National Statistical Office(NSO), under the Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation is responsible for compiling this data.

The Wholesale Price Index(WPI), compiled by Office of the Economic Advisor, Ministry of Commerce and Industry, measure the average change in the prices of goods sold in bulk by wholesale business to other businesses. The WPI is a “producer price” index and does not include services. It is categorized under three components: Primary Articles, fuel, power and Manufactured products.

Unemployment

Unemployment is measure of people who are not working. For the individuals who find themselves without a job, unemployment causes financial hardships.From macroeconomics standpoint, unemployment means society has labor resources that are not fully utilized. Not everyone is who is without a job is unemployed. To be considered employed ,one must be (1) jobless (2) actively seeking work, and (3) able to take a job. Gross Domestic Product(GDP)

GDP is a measure of the size of an economy.A country’s GDP is the dollar value of the final goods & services produced within the country during a year. GDP is the measure of all the automobiles produced that year, all apples grown, total surgeries performed, student educated, and all the new goods and services produced in a current year.

How is GDP measured?

GDP can be measured by adding up all of the items that are purchased in the economy. Purchases are divided into four broad expenditure categories:- (1) consumption spending (2) investment (3) government spending (4) net exports. Consumption spending measures the items that households purchases such as movie tickets, cups of coffee, clothing. Consumption expenditure accounts for about two-third of US GDP.

Investment spending refers primarily to purchases by businesses.It is important to note that in this term investment does not refers to buying stocks and bonds but its refers to purchasing new capital goods, such as buildings and machinery and equipments for business and that will be used to produce other goods. Products that producers make but do not sell this year (and so are not added in consumption spending) are included in this year GDP calculation through investment component. This spending category is roughly 15% to 18% of the US GDP.

Government spending includes spending by federal, state, and local governments.Federal spending would include purchases of new fighter jet and services for military. State governments purchase products such as concrete for new highway project and services such as the work of state troopers. Local governments purchase a variety of goods and services, such as books for city library, playground equipments for the community park.Government spending accounts for nearly 20% of the US GDP.
