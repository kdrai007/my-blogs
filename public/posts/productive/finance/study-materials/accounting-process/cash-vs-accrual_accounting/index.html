Cash vs Accrual Accounting

In this chapter, we will discuss the basic elements of cash and accrual accounting and the businesses that are most likely to use each one. Some private company can choose cash-basis accounting over accrual accounting to report financial information.

Cash-Basis Accounting

Cash basis accounting is a method of accounting in which transactions are not recorded in statements until there is exchange of cash. Cash basis accounting some time impacts the timing of revenue and expenses reporting until cash receipts or outlays occurs. For example, Chris measured the performance of her landscaping business for the month using cash flows. Cash accounting is far simpler then accrual-basis accounting.

  • Pros : It’s straightforward and easy to understand. You get real-time snapshot of your actual cash in hand.It’s great small business, or a roadside fast food stand.
  • Cons : It can be misleading, Your books might show tons of cash, but you could have bunch of unpaid bills or uncollected invoices incoming.It doesn’t give you full picture of company’s financial health.

Accrual-Basis Accounting

This method is more sophisticated. You record income when you earn it(even if you haven’t been paid yet) and expenses when you incur them (even if you haven’t paid the bill yet). It’s based on the matching principals which links revenues to the expanses that generated them.

  • Pros: It provides much more clear financial information of your company. It includes things likes account receivable(money owed to you) and account payable(money you owe).
  • Cons: It’s more complex and required more bookkeeping. You might look profitable on paper but be short on cash because you’re waiting for payments.

Current Assets: CA are those that are consumed within a year. Current Liabilities: CL are those that will be due within a year. Non-current liabilities are those that are due more than year.
