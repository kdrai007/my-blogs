Reliability ^yUCzUzJh

Everybody has an intuitive idea of what it means for something to be reliable or unreliable. For software, typical expectations include:

  • The application perform the function that the user expected.
  • It can tolerate the user making mistakes or using the software in unexpected ways.
  • The systems prevents any unauthorized access and abuse.

The things that can go wrong are called faults, and systems that anticipate faults and can cope with them are called fault-tolerant or resilient.

  1. Hardware Faults ^ie0fYNmT

When we think of cause of system failure, hardware faults quickly comes to mind. Hard disk crash, RAM become faulty , the power grid blackout, someone unplug the network cable etc these problems happens frequently in large data centers. ^WxT0VYw4

Prevention : ^oH3RTGLG

First response is always to add redundancy to the individual hardware component in order to reduce the failure rate of the system.

  • Disks may be set up in a *RAID configuration
  • servers may have dual power supplies and hot-swappble CPUs
  • Datacenters may have batteries and diesel generators for power backup

When one component dies, The redundant component can take its place while the broken problems are recovered. ^BBOwC1iA

  1. Human Errors ^l2WehDG7

Humans design and build software systems, and the operators who keep the systems running are also humans. Even when they have the best intentions, humans are known to be unreliable. for example, study of large internet services found that configuration errors by operators were the leading cause of outages,whereas hardware faults play a role in only 10-25% of outages ^krsVVBoF

Prevention : ^29DSzObR

How do we make out system reliable, in spite of unreliable humans, The best system combine several approaches:

  • Design systems in way that minimizes opportunities for error. For example, well-designed abstractions,APIs, and admin interfaces make it easy to do “the right thing” and discourage “the wrong thing.”

  • Decouple the places where people make the most mistakes from the places where they can failure.

  • Test thoroughly at all levels, from unit tests to whole-systems integration tests and manual test. Automated testing is widely used and especially valuable for covering corner cases that rarely arise in normal operation.

  • Set up detailed and clear monitoring, such as performance metrics and error rates. In other engineering disciplines this is referred to as telemetry . ^gKk0SQxw

Describing Performance ^wJkmAbHB

Once you have described the load on your systems, you can investigate what happens when the load increases. You can look at it in two ways:

  • When you increase a load parameter and keep the system resources(CPU,memory, network bandwidth ,etc) unchanged,how is your system performance affected?

  • When you increase a load parameter, how much you need to increase the resources to keep performance unchanged. ^ebaxtQqW

Illustrating mean and percentiles: response times for a sample of 100 requests to a service ^T4G4SkGp

Calculate response time ^5F6WAq5j

It’s common to see “average response time” reported (Average response time is calculated by , suppose there’s n number add all number up-to n then divide it by n. This is basic mean formula to calculate average) However, the mean is not your average very good metric if you want to know “response time”, because it doesn’t tell you how many users actually experienced that delay.

  • Usually it is better to use percentiles. If you take your list of response time and sort it from fastest to slowest, then the median is the halfway point ^iR33nKgn

Types of scaling ^SkXSRmdh

Horizontal Scaling: Distributing loads across multiple systems.

Vertical Scaling: Upgrade current system to more powerful one. ^aYLcBw1H

Maintainability ^6Djt6L8k

It’s known in industry that the majority of the cost of software is not in its initial development, but in its ongoing maintenance - fixing bugs,keeping its systems operational, investigating failures, adapting it to new platform etc

We should design software in such a way that it will hopefully minimize pain during maintenance, and thus stop creating legacy software ourselves. To achieve this we will pay particular attention to three design principles for software systems: ^QrEAXFiV

Operability ^tDKTK7SF

Make it easy for operations teams to keep the system running smoothly. ^EQxfB8MM

Simplicity ^OZ8wrrOc

Make it easy for new engineers to understand the system, by removing as much complexity as possible from the system ^S2Ua9vzQ

Evolvability ^zSR1zimX

Make it easy for engineers to make changes to the system in the future, adapting it for unanticipated use cases as requirements changes. Also known as extensibility,modifiability,or plasticity ^9VnmcaYe
