# Interest Rates

An interest rate is rental price of money.

### Markets for Loanable Funds.

The concepts of supply,demands and equilibrium apply in this market just like other markets.This markets referred to as the market for loanable funds. In the market of loanable funds, the suppliers of the funds are economic entities that currently have surplus in their budget.Instead of just putting those surplus in a savings box, they decide to lend them to borrowers. In essence, they are renting the money to someone else, who pays the rental price called the *interest rate*.

The suppliers of loanable funds, also known as lenders, are represented by upward-sloping curve in figure. A higher interest rate will encourage lenders to supply larger quantities of funds.

The demander of funds in a loanable funds market are economic entities that currently have a deficit in their budget. They want to spend more then the current income. The downward sloping in figure represent the demander of loanable funds, also known as borrowers. Higher interest rate will be associated with lower quantities demanded for loanable funds and vice-versa.

- Figure: Equilibrium in the loanable fund Market.

The equilibrium interest rate is determined by the intersection of the demand and supply curves. At that interest rate, the quantity supplied of loanable funds exactly equals the quantity demanded of loanable funds. There is no shortage of loanable funds as well nor any surplus.

#### Nominal Interest Rates

The nominal interest rate is the stated, or quoted, interest rate. If you want to borrow money to buy a car and the bank quotes an interest rate of 5.5% on a four year auto loan, the 5.5% is the nominal interest rate. 

`i ≈ r + π`

Where, 

i = Nominal interest rate. 
r = Real interest rate
π = Inflation rate

#### Real Interest Rates

The real interest rate is a fundamental economic concept that measure the rate of return of an investment or the cost of borrowings after adjusting for the effects of inflation (π). It provides a more accurate reflection of the true economic return than the nominal interest rate (i).  

`r ≈  i - π`

Where, 

r = Real interest rate
i = Nominal interest rate. 
π = Inflation rate

#### Risk Premiums

In finance, a **risk premiums** is the excess return an investment is expected to yield over the **risk-free rate of return**. It is a core concept that reflect the fundamental principal that a rational, risk-averse investor will demand greater compensation for bearing higher risk.
